Introducing my cat

Curiously leaning in, tilting my head for a closer look, I examined its coat. It wasn't pure black, but a mix of gray and brown, with touches of orange and white. Using a color picker, I got "#BDC4CC, #393833, #AA9169" — so it turned out to be a calico cat.

Originally it belonged to a friend, but since my friend was willing to give it away, I thought: let's take it home!

As soon as we got home, it ran off. I kept searching, curious where it had gone. After a long and persistent search, I spotted it hiding inside the wardrobe.

On the way to the vet, it changed. At home it was calm, but outside it became terrified and fearful. In the car, it scratched and thrashed — I got my hand scratched. The restraint bag, originally meant to leave an oxygen opening, became its escape hope. It bit and clawed again and again, destroying the bag.

When we arrived, it sprang out and ran straight away. At that moment I thought: in this huge neighborhood, if I can't find it, it will become a stray — bullied and mistreated. I was determined to find it.

It fled, I chased. The moving shadow under the car, the fleeting dark shape on the stairs — those were my best clues. After all my efforts, following those shadows, it finally stopped. I quickly took it in for a check‑up.

During the examination, I noticed that unlike other cats — who scream and hiss in fear — it didn't. It only used its paws to show its fear. Was it unable to do otherwise, or did something in its early upbringing cause this? Watching the tubes and its pained expression, I kept trying to distract it. The frantic struggling and the blood that flowed out felt like a declaration of a broken bond.

Back home, it seemed fine at first, but then I discovered it had miscarried. It ate the embryo.

A few days later, it became gentle and well‑behaved.
